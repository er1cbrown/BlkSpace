### 2. The Name: "Obelisk of the Sun"
The name can be a little confusing because it's not a true Egyptian-style obelisk. It's more accurately a large, rectangular stone pillar covered in intricate carvings. It was named by archaeologists because of its direct association with the Pyramid of the Sun, the largest structure in Teotihuacan.

### 3. Significance and Carvings
This is where the Obelisk becomes incredibly important. Its four sides are covered in beautifully preserved bas-relief carvings that provide a unique glimpse into the worldview and religion of the Teotihuacan people, whose writing system remains largely undeciphered.

*   **Water and Fertility Symbols:** The most prominent carvings are stylized representations of **water** (often shown as shells and droplets) and **fertility** (represented by feathered heads, possibly of a water or earth goddess). This suggests the obelisk was a powerful symbol of life-giving forces and agricultural abundance.
*   **The "Great Goddess":** Many scholars believe the central figure represented is a key deity in Teotihuacan religion, often referred to as the **"Great Goddess"** or the "Goddess of Water, Fertility, and the Earth." The obelisk may be a representation of her divine power.

### 4. Why Was It Buried?
The obelisk was found in a sacred tunnel or cave beneath the Pyramid of the Sun. This was not an act of discard but a deliberate **ritual deposit**. In Mesoamerican beliefs, caves were considered entrances to the underworld and wombs of the Earth. Burying such a sacred object at the very foundation of the pyramid was likely meant to consecrate the site and channel the earth's spiritual power into the structure above it.
