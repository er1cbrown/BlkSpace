Once dressed, Jeff retrieves their old, battered Model T Ford. As they drive slowly through the familiar countryside where they have worked for 45 years, both are consumed by fear and second thoughts. The journey is a silent pilgrimage past their life: they see the land they cultivated, reflect on their perpetual debt to the plantation owner, Major Stevenson, and recall the devastating loss of all five of their grown children within two years. They even pass the house of a local woman, Delia, taking a small, bitter pride in her seeing them in their finery.

During the drive, Jennie breaks down crying, not out of fear of their plan, but out of sadness for "leavin' eve'thing... we been used to." Jeff also grapples with intense terror, momentarily pleading, "Jennie, I can't do it. I can't." However, after remembering his physical pain, the certainty of more debt, and the terrifying prospect of another stroke that would leave him a helpless burden on his blind wife, he finds a grim resolve. He drives the car off a cliff into a roaring river below, committing joint suicide to escape a life of relentless hardship and to die on their own terms.

**Key Themes:**

*   **Despair and Hopelessness:** The driving force of the story is the utter hopelessness of the couple's situation within the oppressive sharecropping system. This system has left them with no financial future, no family, and only the prospect of increased suffering, making their drastic decision a final act of control.
*   **Dignity in the Face of Adversity:** Their suicide is not presented as an act of cowardice but as a solemn, mutual decision to exert autonomy over their fate. The careful ritual of dressing up shows their desire to meet death with respect and dignity, which life has denied them.
*   **The Brutality of Sharecropping:** The story is a powerful indictment of the economic system that entraps and exhausts generations of Black farmers. Jeff reflects that the landowner's "notion" has killed both mules and men, highlighting the dehumanizing cycle of debt and poverty.
*   **Love and Loyalty:** The deep, tender bond between Jeff and Jennie is central. Their plan is a mutual one, made out of love to spare each other further suffering. Their gentle, supportive dialogue throughout the preparation underscores their lifelong partnership and shared burden.
*   **The Past vs. The Present:** Jeff's brief flashback to a vibrant Mardi Gras trip in his youth contrasts sharply with his current reality, emphasizing how a lifetime of grueling labor and loss has worn them down to shadows of their former selves. The past is a place of memory, but it offers no escape from the present's grim reality.
