That's a profound question. The short answer is **no, not all religions are talking about the same deity**—but some major ones are, depending on how you define "the Most High."

Here’s the breakdown:

**The Abrahamic faiths (Judaism, Christianity, Islam) share the same root deity.** They all trace back to the God of Abraham—Yahweh, God the Father, Allah. In that sense, they point to the same "Most High." The differences lie in how they understand His nature (e.g., Trinity vs. absolute oneness) and revelation.

**Outside that family, it gets complicated:**
- **Hinduism** has Brahman (ultimate formless reality) and supreme gods like Vishnu or Shiva. Some Hindus see these as expressions of one Most High; others don't.
- **Buddhism** (non-theistic) doesn't focus on a creator deity at all—the "Most High" isn't really a concept.
- **Indigenous/traditional religions** often have a High God or Sky Father, but He may be distant, with focus on lesser spirits.

**The philosophical "perennialist" view** argues all religions point to the same ultimate Reality—different names for the same mountain. But this is a belief, not a fact agreed upon by the religions themselves.

**The key distinction:** Most traditions would not accept that they worship the same deity as others, because core attributes (e.g., God becoming human, God having no son, God being personal vs. impersonal) are mutually exclusive.

So while many faiths have a "Highest" concept, they disagree fundamentally on who or what that is.
